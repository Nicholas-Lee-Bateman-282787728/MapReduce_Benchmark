                                January 1 &ndash; Kristiania, the capital of Norway, reverted to its original name of Oslo. January 3 &ndash; Benito Mussolini made a pivotal speech in the Italian Chamber of Deputies. He took personal responsibility for the actions of his Blackshirts, challenged his political opponents to remove him from office and then promised to take charge of restoring order to Italy within forty-eight hours. Historians now trace this speech to the beginning of Mussolini's dictatorship. January 5 &ndash; Nellie Tayloe Ross became the first female governor (Wyoming) in the United States. Twelve days later, Ma Ferguson became first female governor of Texas. January 25 - Hjalmar Branting resigns as Prime Minister of Sweden because of ill health, and is replaced by the minister of trade, Rickard Sandler . January 27&ndash;February 1 &ndash; The 1925 serum run to Nome (the "Great Race of Mercy") relayed diphtheria antitoxin by dog sled across the U.S. territory of Alaska, to combat an epidemic. February 15 &ndash; The Alice Comedy Alice Solves the Puzzle was released by Disney Brothers Cartoon Studio, introducing Bootleg Pete (an early prototype for Pegleg Pete) for the first time. February 21 &ndash; *This is the cover date of the very first issue of The New Yorker, though not necessarily the publication date, as magazines usually date their covers ahead of time. February 25 &ndash; Art Gillham recorded for Columbia Records the first Western Electric masters to be commercially released. February 28 &ndash; The 1925 Charlevoix–Kamouraska earthquake struck northeastern North America. March 4 İsmet İnönü was appointed as the prime minister in Turkey (Turkey's 4th and İnönü's 3rd government). The inauguration of Calvin Coolidge as President of the United States was the first to be broadcast on radio. March 6 &ndash; Pionerskaya Pravda, one of the oldest children's newspapers in Europe, was founded in the Soviet Union. March 9&ndash;May 1 &ndash; Pink's War: The British Royal Air Force bombarded mountain strongholds of Mahsud tribesmen in South Waziristan. March 15 &ndash; The Phi Lambda Chi fraternity (original name "The Aztecs") was founded on the campus of Arkansas State Teacher's College in Conway, Arkansas (now the University of Central Arkansas). March 18 &ndash; The Tri-State Tornado, the deadliest in U.S. history, rampaged through Missouri, Illinois, and Indiana, killing 695 people and injuring 2,027. It hit the towns of Murphysboro, Illinois; Gorham, Illinois; Ellington, Missouri; and Griffin, Indiana. March 21 &ndash; Tennessee Governor Austin Peay signed the Butler Act, prohibiting the teaching of evolution in the state's public schools. March 31 &ndash; Radio station WOWO in Fort Wayne, Indiana began broadcasting. April&ndash;October &ndash; The Exposition Internationale des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels Modernes is held in Paris, giving a name to the Art Deco style. April 1 Frank Heath and his horse Gypsy Queen left Washington, D.C. to begin a two-year journey to visit all 48 states. The Patent and Trademark Office was transferred to the Department of Commerce. April 10 &ndash; F. Scott Fitzgerald published The Great Gatsby. April 15 &ndash; Fritz Haarmann, a serial killer convicted of the murder of 24 boys and young men, was beheaded in Germany. April 16 &ndash; The Communist assault on St. Nedelya Church claims roughly 150 lives in Sofia, Bulgaria. April 20 &ndash; Iranian forces of Rezā Shāh occupied Ahvaz, and arrested Sheikh Khaz'al. April 28 &ndash; Presenting the Stanley Baldwin government's budget, Chancellor of the Exchequer Winston Churchill announced Britain's return to the gold standard. May 1 &ndash; The mausoleums in al-Baqi were destroyed by King Ibn Saud (Lanati). In the same year, he also demolishes the tombs of holy persons at Mualla Cemetery in Mecca where Muhammad's first wife Khadijah, his grandfather and other ancestors are buried. This happened despite protests by the international Islamic community. May 5 Scopes Trial: Dayton, Tennessee, biology teacher John Scopes was arrested for teaching Charles Darwin's Theory of Evolution. The General Election Law was passed in Japan. May 8 &ndash; Tom Lee rescued 32 people from the M.E. Norman, a sinking steamboat. May 25 Scopes Trial: John T. Scopes was indicted for teaching Darwin's theory of evolution. The National Forensic League was founded. May 29 &ndash; British explorer Percy Fawcett sent a last telegram to his wife, before he disappeared in the Amazon. June 1 &ndash; Percy and Florence Arrowsmith were married. This couple, who celebrated their 80th wedding anniversary June 1, 2005 (Percy aged 105, and wife Florence 100), are acknowledged by the Guinness Book of Records as record-holders for the longest marriage for a living couple and the greatest aggregate age of a married couple. June 6 &ndash; The Chrysler Corporation was founded by Walter Percy Chrysler. June 13 &ndash; Charles Francis Jenkins achieved the first synchronized transmission of pictures and sound, using 48 lines, and a mechanical system. A 10-minute film of a miniature windmill in motion was sent across 5 miles from Anacostia to Washington, D.C. The images were viewed by representatives of the National Bureau of Standards, the U.S. Navy, the Commerce Department, and others. Jenkins calls this "the first public demonstration of radiovision". June 14 The Aristotle University of Thessaloniki in Greece was founded. The Turkish football club Göztepe was founded. June 29 &ndash; 1925 Santa Barbara earthquake: A 6.8 earthquake destroyed downtown Santa Barbara, California. July 9 &ndash; In Dublin, Ireland, Oonagh Keogh became the first female member of a stock exchange in the world. July 10 Scopes Trial: In Dayton, Tennessee, the so-called "Monkey Trial" began with John T. Scopes, a young high school science teacher, accused of teaching evolution in violation of a Tennessee state law. Meher Baba begins his 44-year silence. July 18 &ndash; Adolf Hitler published Volume 1 of his personal manifesto Mein Kampf. July 21 &ndash; Scopes Trial: In Dayton, Tennessee, high school biology teacher John T. Scopes was found guilty of teaching evolution in class and fined $100. July 25 &ndash; The Telegraph Agency of the Soviet Union (TASS) was established. August 8 &ndash; The Ku Klux Klan demonstrated its popularity by holding a parade in Washington DC; as many as 40,000 male and female members of the Klan march down Pennsylvania Avenue. In 1925, an estimated 5,000,000 members belong to the Ku Klux Klan, making it the largest fraternal organization in the United States. August 14 &ndash; The original Hetch Hetchy Moccasin Powerhouse was completed and goes on line. August 25 &ndash; The French completed their evacuation of the Ruhr region of Germany. September 3 &ndash; The U.S. Navy dirigible Shenandoah broke up in a squall line near Caldwell, Ohio; 14 crewmen are killed. September 27 &ndash; Feast of the Cross according to the Old Calendar; A celestial cross appeared over Athens, Greece, while the Greek police pursues a group of Greek Old Calendarists. The phenomenon lasted for half an hour. October &ndash; The major money forgery and fraud of Alves dos Reis was exposed in Portugal. October 1 &ndash; Mount Rushmore National Memorial was dedicated in South Dakota. October 2 &ndash; In London John Logie Baird successfully transmitted the first television pictures with a greyscale image. The city's first enclosed double-decker buses were introduced. October 5&ndash;16 &ndash; The Locarno Treaties were negotiated. October 8 &ndash; Cubana de Aviación was founded. November 5 &ndash; Secret agent Sidney Reilly is executed by the OGPU, the secret police of the Soviet Union. November 14 &ndash; The first Surrealist art exhibition opened in Paris. November 24 &ndash; The silent film El Húsar de la Muerte was released in Santiago, Chile. November 26 &ndash; Prajadhipok (Rama VII) was crowned as King of Siam. November 28 &ndash; The weekly country music-variety radio program Grand Ole Opry was first broadcast on WSM radio in Nashville, Tennessee, as the "WSM Barn Dance". December 1 &ndash; The Locarno Treaties were signed in London. December 11 &ndash; Pope Pius XI's encyclical Quas primas, on the Feast of Christ the King, is promulgated. December 16 Reza Shah became shah of Persia. Alpha Phi Omega, a National service fraternity, was founded at Lafayette College. Colombo Radio launched in Ceylon; the station was subsequently known as Radio Ceylon. December 25 &ndash; IG Farben was formed by the merger of six chemical companies in Germany. Spring &ndash; Leica I 35 mm film still camera introduced. The Australian state of Queensland introduced a 44-hour working week. The Brisbane City Council, (Brisbane, Australia), was created from the amalgamation of 20 smaller cities, towns and shires. New York City became the largest city in the world, taking the lead from London. The Thompson submachine gun sold for $175 in the 1925 Sears, Roebuck and Company mail order catalog. The National Football League added 5 teams: the New York Giants, Detroit Panthers, Providence Steam Roller, a new Canton Bulldogs team, and the Pottsville Maroons. In Germany, the Bauhaus moved to a building in Dessau designed by Walter Gropius. Lion Feuchtwanger's novel Jud Süß published. The Shueisha Publishing Company was founded in Tokyo. The Wheel gymnastics was invented in Germany.
